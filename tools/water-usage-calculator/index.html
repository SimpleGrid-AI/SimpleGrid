Why water is the line item nobody questions

Most manufacturing CFOs can quote you their kWh price within a cent. Almost none can tell you their water rate or which process burns the most. That's because the water bill is small enough to ignore (until it isn't) and the meter sits at the main, not at each process. Sub-meter your top 3 users and the savings show up inside a year.

Water and sewer are two separate meters - and rates

Most utilities charge a water rate (delivery) and a sewer rate (treatment of what goes back). Sewer is usually higher than water because treatment costs more than supply. Some processes - cooling tower evaporation, water that ends up in product - never reach the sewer; for those, ask your utility about a sewer deduction or evaporation credit. Submitted with even a basic meter log, it can cut your effective rate 20-40%.

Recirculation: where the real savings live

Cooling towers, CNC coolant, wash-water, boiler condensate - all four routinely waste 60-90% of throughput. Closed-loop chiller systems, conductivity-controlled cooling tower blowdown, coolant skimmers, and condensate return tanks pay back fast in any high-water operation. The hard part isn't the equipment - it's knowing which process is bleeding gallons. That's what this calculator surfaces.

Frequently asked questions

How do I estimate water usage by process without sub-meters?

Start with nameplate / equipment manuals (cooling towers and washers publish gpm). For sanitary, multiply employee count by 20 gal/day. For wash-down, time a hose at known psi for one minute. Sub-meters cost $200-800 installed and pay back within months on any process over 1000 gal/day.

What recirculation rates are realistic?

Cooling tower blowdown: 70-90% recovery is standard with proper conductivity control. CNC coolant: 80-95% with skimmers/filters. Wash-water: 50-70% with filtration. Boiler condensate return: 80-95%. Sanitary water cannot be recirculated.

Is sewer always charged separately?

Almost always, and sewer rates are often higher than water rates because they include treatment. Some utilities cap sewer charges based on incoming water (assuming most goes down the drain); for processes where water doesn't return (cooling tower evaporation, product moisture), you can sometimes apply for sewer deductions.

Do I need to treat process water before discharge?

Yes if you discharge anything other than sanitary - metal finishing, paint shops, food rinse, chemical processes all require pretreatment under EPA Categorical Pretreatment Standards. Your POTW (publicly owned treatment works) issues an industrial discharge permit with specific limits.

Are there water-efficiency rebates?

In water-stressed states (CA, AZ, TX, CO) utilities and water districts offer rebates for cooling tower upgrades, cleaning/sanitizing system retrofits, and process recirculation projects - often $1-5 per gallon/day saved. Check with your local water utility before sizing any project.
