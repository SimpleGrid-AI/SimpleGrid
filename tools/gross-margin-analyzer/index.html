Why per-product margin matters more than total margin

"Our gross margin is 32%" is almost meaningless without the breakdown. The actual distribution for a typical mid-market manufacturer: a handful of products run at 45-55% margin and generate most of the profit. A larger middle tier runs at 25-35%. And a tail of products runs at 5-15% (or negative), consuming capacity that could be running something better.

That 32% blended number tells you nothing about the lever. Per-product margin tells you exactly what to fix.

The four quadrants - what to do

  • Stars (high volume + high margin). Protect at all costs. Invest in capacity, marketing, and customer relationships. These products pay the rent. Make sure your salespeople know what these are - and aren't accidentally discounting them.
  • Cash Cows (high volume + low margin). Volume is there but margin is thin. Two levers: raise prices (you have volume - test elasticity) or reduce cost (process improvement, material substitution). Even a 2% price increase on a Cash Cow is more profit than a 20% increase on a Niche Winner.
  • Niche Winners (low volume + high margin). The hidden gems. Sales effort here pays off enormously - each new customer is high-margin. Investigate why volume is low: poor marketing? limited awareness? capacity constraint? customer education?
  • Dogs (low volume + low margin). Three options - raise prices (and lose the volume? fine), reduce cost (often impossible at low volume), or discontinue (free up capacity). Audit each Dog for strategic reason. If none exists, discontinue.

The unprofitable-product trap

"But this product covers overhead!" - the most common defense of unprofitable products. It's almost always wrong. Overhead is fixed in the short term - you pay it whether the product runs or not. What an unprofitable product DOES do is consume scarce capacity that could be running a profitable product. Every hour the bottleneck spends on a Dog is an hour not spent on a Star.

Frequently asked questions

What is a whale curve?

A cumulative profit chart sorted from most to least profitable product. It typically rises steeply (top products), levels off (middle), then falls (unprofitable bottom). Shape looks like a whale.

How should I handle unprofitable products?

Three options: raise prices, reduce cost, or discontinue. The wrong answer is keeping them at current price/cost because "they cover overhead" - they don't, they destroy contribution.

What's the difference between gross margin and contribution margin?

Gross margin includes all COGS (variable + allocated overhead). Contribution margin includes only variable costs. Contribution for short-term mix decisions, gross margin for portfolio decisions.

What are the four quadrants?

Stars (high vol + high margin), Cash Cows (high vol + low margin), Niche Winners (low vol + high margin), Dogs (low vol + low margin).

Should I always discontinue Dog products?

Not always. Some Dogs are strategic - they're loss leaders for a key customer or part of a product family. But a Dog with no strategic purpose is just consuming capacity that could run a Star.
