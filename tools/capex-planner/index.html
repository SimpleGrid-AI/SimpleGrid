Why one-at-a-time capex decisions go wrong

Each piece of equipment, evaluated alone, almost always makes sense - that's why the vendor brought you the quote. The problem is that "yes" decisions stack. Three "yes" decisions over a year that each consume 12% of EBITDA become a 36% capex year, before you've counted maintenance. By the time the third loan officer asks for the third DSCR proforma, you've discovered something your one-at-a-time process missed.

A 5-year plan forces sequencing. You can still buy the third machine - just maybe in Year 3 instead of Year 1. The tradeoff becomes visible and decidable.

The tax side

  • Section 179. Up to ~$1.16M (2024) of qualifying equipment can be deducted in Year 1 instead of spread over 5-7 years. For a 25% taxpayer, that's a $290K-of-savings front-load.
  • Bonus depreciation. 60% in 2024, 40% in 2025, 20% in 2026, 0% in 2027 (unless Congress acts). Apply to whatever Section 179 didn't cover.
  • Timing matters. Section 179 applies to the year equipment is placed in service, not purchased. A December order delivered January 2 moves the deduction to next year. Plan accordingly.

The financing side

Most mid-market manufacturers underuse equipment financing for ego reasons ("we pay cash"). The math usually says otherwise: tax-deductible interest below 9% on equipment that earns 25%+ ROI is positive arbitrage. Preserving cash for working capital or opportunistic acquisitions is almost always more valuable than tying it up in steel.

The exception: distressed credit conditions, very short useful lives, or specific tax planning windows where Section 179 isn't usable. Pair this with our Lease vs Buy calculator for individual decisions.

Frequently asked questions

Why plan capex five years out?

Because equipment decisions are 5-10 year commitments and they stack on top of each other. A 5-year view lets you sequence purchases against cash, debt capacity, and tax planning.

What is a typical capex spend for a mid-market manufacturer?

Maintenance capex is 3-5% of revenue annually. Growth capex adds 2-8% during expansion. A $20M manufacturer in growth mode might run $1.5M-$2M/year.

How does Section 179 affect capex planning?

Section 179 lets you deduct up to ~$1.16M of qualifying equipment in Year 1 instead of depreciating over 5-7 years. That tax savings often makes financing cheaper than paying cash and changes the optimal mix of cash vs. loan vs. lease.

What is the funding gap?

The difference between your capex plan in a given year and what your operating cash flow + borrowing capacity can support. If you have a gap, defer, finance, or raise capital.

Should I buy or lease?

Depends on tax position, cash position, and useful life. Buying captures Section 179 and ownership equity but uses cash. Leasing preserves cash and is cleaner for short-lived or technology-obsolescent equipment.
