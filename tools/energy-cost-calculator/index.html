Energy is the bill no one analyzes

Most manufacturers know their electricity rate within a tenth of a cent but cannot tell you which machine costs the most to run. Energy is paid as a single monthly number, never broken down to the asset, never tied to the job. That is fine when energy is 1% of revenue. It is a problem when it's 4-6% and growing - which is increasingly common as compressed air, HVAC, and process heat loads grow with capacity. The first step to controlling energy cost is breaking it apart. Once you know where the kWh go, the ROI math on upgrades becomes obvious.

The three lines on your bill

Commercial electric bills usually have three components: kWh charge (energy you used), demand charge (the highest 15-minute peak in the month, charged $/kW), and fixed fees. Demand charges alone can be 30-50% of the bill - which means staggering startup of large motors saves real money without changing total energy consumption. Natural gas is separate but worth tracking, especially if you run process heat (ovens, furnaces, paint booths) or use gas for facility heating.

The three upgrades with proven ROI

VFD on rotary screw compressors cuts compressor energy 25-35% (most shops' #2 energy consumer). LED lighting retrofit cuts lighting energy 50-70% with 2-3 year payback. Fixing compressed air leaks - which average 20-30% of compressor output in unmaintained shops - is free to start and pays back in months. These three moves alone often cut energy spend 20-30% with under $30K capex for a small shop. They also cut Scope 2 emissions, which OEMs are increasingly asking about.

Frequently asked questions

How accurate are the equipment power-draw defaults?

Defaults are midpoint nameplate ratings from manufacturer datasheets across common makes. Actual draw can vary 20-40% based on motor age, load profile, and condition. For binding numbers, put a clamp meter on each circuit or pull data from your power monitoring system. The defaults are good enough to rank machines and prioritize where to measure.

What is a duty cycle and how do I estimate it?

Duty cycle is the percentage of time a machine is actually drawing rated power while powered on. A CNC running 24/7 might be cutting only 60% of the time (idling, tool changes, part loads in between). A welder might be 20-30%. An air compressor often runs 70-85%. If you have no data, 70-80% is a reasonable starting point for most production equipment.

What are demand charges?

Most commercial utilities bill two things: kWh (energy used) and kW demand (the highest 15-minute peak in the billing period). Demand charges can be $8-25 per kW per month and often equal 30-50% of the total bill. Running 3 big machines simultaneously instead of staggered can spike your peak demand and push your bill up for the whole month, even if total kWh is identical.

How does my energy spend compare to other manufacturers?

Energy as a percentage of revenue is the best benchmark. Light assembly: 0.5-1.5%. Machining + fabrication: 1.5-3%. Heavy metals, foundry, plastics, food processing: 3-8%. Above 5% in a discrete machine shop usually means an air leak problem, oversized compressor, or HVAC running outside production hours.

What's the highest-ROI energy upgrade?

For most shops with rotary screw compressors: a Variable Frequency Drive (VFD) retrofit cuts compressor energy 25-35% with 12-24 month payback. Second: LED lighting retrofit (50-70% reduction, 2-3 year payback). Third: fix compressed air leaks - the average shop loses 20-30% of compressor output to leaks, costing thousands per year. None of these require capex over $30K for most small shops.
