Why prototype cost feels random

You email a CAD file to three shops and get back quotes that vary 3x. One quotes $80/part, one quotes $250/part, one quotes $40/part for what looks like the same job. The reason is not dishonesty - it is that each shop is pricing the part with their own machine load, material on hand, fixturing preferences, and tolerance interpretation. Knowing the rough cost band before you ask makes those quotes intelligible and lets you push back on the outliers.

Pick the cheapest method that meets your test goals

The most common prototype mistake is over-specifying. You CNC a part in 7075-T6 to validate that two surfaces clear each other - a $3 3D print would have answered the same question. Use the most expensive method only when the cheaper method physically cannot answer your test question (functional load, real tolerance, true material, sealing, thermal). Iterate cheap, validate once expensive.

Break-even between prototyping and production

Prototype methods scale linearly with quantity. Production tooling (injection mold, progressive die, casting tool) has a steep up-front cost but a low per-unit cost. The crossover - the quantity at which tooling pays for itself - is typically 500-3,000 units depending on tooling cost and part complexity. Below crossover: prototype methods or soft tooling. Above: invest in production tooling. The tool above estimates your specific crossover.

Frequently asked questions

Why is CNC so much more expensive than 3D printing?

CNC machining starts from a solid block and removes material with carbide tooling on a $100K+ machine run by a $35-50/hr operator. Programming, setup, and fixturing add fixed cost regardless of quantity. 3D printing has near-zero setup, runs unattended, and starts from $20-100/kg material. For a one-off plastic bracket, FDM can be 10-30x cheaper than CNC. For a tight-tolerance metal part, CNC is the only option.

When does production tooling beat prototyping?

Hard tooling (steel injection mold, progressive die) is $25K-$150K up front but drops per-unit cost to $0.50-$5. Break-even vs CNC at $50/part is around 500-3,000 units depending on tooling cost. Below that quantity, prototype methods win. Above that, hard tooling wins. Soft tooling (silicone mold, urethane cast) bridges the gap from qty 25-200 plastic parts.

Can I trust 3D-printed parts for functional testing?

For form-fit checks and low-load functional tests, yes. For load-bearing, sealing, or thermal cycling, no - FDM is layer-anisotropic (weak across layers), SLS is porous, SLA is brittle. Use 3D prints to validate geometry, then machine or mold the test specimen for true functional validation.

What is soft tooling or bridge tooling?

Silicone molds (RTV) cast from a 3D-printed master. You then cast urethane parts into the mold - 25-50 shots before the mold wears out. $1,500-$3,500 tooling cost, $20-40/part. Ideal for plastic prototype runs of 25-200 where CNC is too expensive and injection molding is not worth the tooling investment yet.

Should I prototype overseas to save money?

Overseas CNC (typically China, India) is 30-60% cheaper but adds 2-3 weeks of shipping, customs risk, and a tighter feedback loop. For first prototypes where you will iterate, the time cost usually outweighs the dollar savings. For locked designs in larger prototype quantities, overseas is reasonable. Always factor in revision cycles.
