Why allocation method matters

Manufacturing overhead is real money - rent, utilities, depreciation, supervision, insurance. Typically 15-30% of revenue. Allocating it to products is unavoidable because that's how you compute true cost, true margin, and defensible pricing. The question is: which allocation base?

A single plant-wide rate is the most common choice because it's simple. It's also frequently wrong. If your shop runs both labor-intensive assembly work and machine-intensive CNC work, a plant-wide labor-hour rate over-allocates overhead to the assembly products (they have more labor hours) and under-allocates to the CNC products (they have fewer labor hours but heavier machine consumption). The price you quote on each looks fine - but the assembly customer is subsidizing the CNC customer.

When to use which

  • Direct labor hours. Works when overhead is dominated by labor-related costs (supervision, indirect labor, HR-related expense) and labor hours are uniform across products.
  • Direct labor cost. Slight variant - useful when labor rates vary significantly across product types (skilled assembly vs. unskilled packing).
  • Machine hours. Better when overhead is dominated by machine-related costs (equipment depreciation, energy, machine maintenance, capacity-driven overhead).
  • Departmental rates. Different rate per department or work center. Best when your shop has a mix of labor-heavy and machine-heavy operations.
  • Activity-based costing. Trace overhead to activities (setup, material handling, inspection), then to products by activity consumption. Most accurate, highest effort.

Frequently asked questions

Why does the allocation method matter?

Because a single plant-wide rate spreads ALL overhead across ONE base. If product mix has different overhead drivers, you over/under-allocate and price decisions get made on bad costs.

What allocation base should I use?

It depends on what drives the overhead. Machine-intensive shops: machine hours. Labor-intensive shops: labor hours. Mixed shops: departmental allocation.

Is activity-based costing (ABC) worth it?

For complex product mixes with high overhead, yes. More accurate, more work to maintain. Most mid-market manufacturers benefit from departmental allocation as a middle ground.

What if my numbers differ a lot between methods?

That's the insight. Big swings mean your overhead drivers are not uniform across products - a single plant-wide rate is misleading.

Can I just use machine hours since we're a machine shop?

If 70%+ of your overhead is machine-driven and products use machines proportionally, yes. Otherwise even a "pure machine shop" has labor-heavy and machine-heavy products.
