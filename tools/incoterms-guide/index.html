Why Incoterms matter more than your sales contract

Every international shipment has 10+ handoff points - the seller's loading dock, export customs, the carrier, ocean main carriage, destination port, import customs, final delivery. At each point, someone is responsible for the cost. At each point, someone bears the risk of loss or damage. Incoterms are the 11 three-letter shorthand codes that map those handoffs unambiguously. Get the term wrong and you'll be arguing with your buyer over a damaged container, an unexpected duty bill, or an uninsured loss six months after the goods shipped.

The 11 terms split into 4 families

The E term (EXW): seller does the least, buyer does everything. The F terms (FCA, FAS, FOB): seller delivers to a named place in the country of origin, buyer arranges main carriage. The C terms (CFR, CIF, CPT, CIP): seller arranges and pays for main carriage to destination, but risk still transfers at origin (counter-intuitive - a CIF buyer bears risk during ocean transit even though seller paid for the freight). The D terms (DAP, DPU, DDP): seller delivers all the way to destination and bears risk through arrival.

Ocean-only vs any-mode

Four terms are ocean-only: FAS, FOB, CFR, CIF. They reference the ship's rail and named ports. Seven terms work for any mode (truck, rail, air, ocean, multimodal): EXW, FCA, CPT, CIP, DAP, DPU, DDP. If your container moves on truck-rail-ocean-truck, FOB technically does not apply - use FCA or CPT/CIP instead. This is one of the most common mistakes manufacturers make.

Frequently asked questions

What's the difference between FOB Incoterms and FOB UCC?

Under Incoterms 2020, FOB (Free On Board) applies only to ocean/inland waterway transport, with risk transferring when goods are loaded on the vessel at the named port. Under the US Uniform Commercial Code (UCC), 'FOB' is used much more broadly - 'FOB origin' or 'FOB destination' for any mode (truck, rail, etc.) and primarily addresses cost responsibility, not risk transfer at a port. Always specify 'FOB [port], Incoterms 2020' on international contracts to avoid ambiguity.

When should I use DDP?

Use DDP (Delivered Duty Paid) when your buyer has no international logistics capability and you want a single all-in delivered price. The seller handles everything: freight, export clearance, import duties, taxes, delivery to the buyer's door. Risky for the seller because you're on the hook for unfamiliar import rules and taxes. Best for: e-commerce, samples, low-value shipments, and buyers that explicitly refuse to act as importer of record.

Do Incoterms cover title transfer?

No. Incoterms cover three things: who pays for what, who bears risk of loss/damage at each stage, and who handles customs clearance. They do NOT govern title (ownership) transfer, payment terms, or contract law. Title transfer must be addressed separately in your sales contract - typically at payment, at shipment, or at delivery depending on jurisdiction.

What's new in Incoterms 2020 vs 2010?

Key changes: (1) DAT was renamed DPU (Delivered at Place Unloaded) to clarify that any destination - not just a terminal - is valid. (2) Insurance levels under CIP were raised from minimum (Clause C) to all-risks (Clause A); CIF stayed at minimum. (3) FCA was updated to allow on-board bill of lading for sellers needing letters of credit. (4) Security-related obligations were clarified across all terms.

Are Incoterms legally binding?

Incoterms become legally binding when both parties explicitly reference them in the sales contract - e.g., 'CIF Long Beach, Incoterms 2020'. They are published by the International Chamber of Commerce (ICC) and are recognized worldwide. Without explicit reference, courts may interpret common terms like 'FOB' under local law (e.g., UCC in the US), which can differ from ICC definitions.
