Why an SLA helps both sides

For the supplier, an SLA establishes clarity about what good looks like - the team knows the targets, the customer knows the targets, and arguments about "what we expected" go away. For the customer, the SLA replaces vague expectations with measurable commitments and a defined escalation path. The combination of explicit targets, periodic reporting, and structured reviews catches problems while they're small and creates documented trends. Most disputes between manufacturers and customers come from misaligned expectations - an SLA fixes that.

How to pick targets you can actually hit

Use trailing 12 months of actual performance as a baseline. If your real OTD is 92%, don't commit to 98%. Better to commit to 93% and beat it consistently than 98% and miss every quarter. Same for PPM - if you're running 8,000, commit to 7,500 and improve. The point of an SLA isn't to make stretch goals public; it's to set expectations you can consistently meet and to create the accountability framework for improving them over time.
