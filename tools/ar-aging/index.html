Why aging matters more than total AR

$2M of receivables sounds the same on a balance sheet whether it's all 25 days old or all 120 days old. In reality, they are wildly different positions. The 25-day version becomes cash next month. The 120-day version is half write-off, half wishful thinking. The aging breakdown is the only way to tell them apart.

The standard 5-bucket aging (Current / 31-60 / 61-90 / 91-120 / 120+) is the format every accountant, banker, and PE diligence team will ask for. Running it monthly is the absolute minimum for a mid-market manufacturer.

The probability-of-collection curve

Industry data is brutal on overdue AR. Roughly: current invoices collect at 99%, 31-60 day at 93%, 61-90 at 80%, 91-120 at 65%, 120+ at 45%. Past 180 days the curve drops below 25%. The math: every day past 90, money walks out the door.

This is why "I'll call them next week" is so expensive. Each week of delay at days 60-120 cuts the collection probability by roughly 2-3 percentage points. On a $50K invoice, that's $1,000-$1,500 per week of inaction.

The standard reserve framework

The bad debt reserve estimates the portion of AR you should expect to write off. Standard starting points by bucket:

  • Current (0-30): 1% - friction, occasional disputes
  • 31-60: 5% - some real collection risk emerging
  • 61-90: 15% - elevated risk, often dispute or distress
  • 91-120: 30% - meaningful chunk will not collect in-house
  • 120+: 50% - half is gone unless you escalate

Adjust these based on your own write-off history. A manufacturer with strong credit policies and government customers might cut reserves in half. One with broad consumer-facing distribution or international customers might double them.

Frequently asked questions

What aging buckets are standard?

The GAAP-standard buckets are Current (0-30 days), 31-60, 61-90, 91-120, and 120+. The 5-bucket split is more useful for collection prioritization than a 4-bucket version.

What percent of my AR should be current?

For a healthy manufacturer with Net 30 terms: 75-85% in Current, 10-15% in 31-60, less than 5% in 90+. If more than 15% is over 60 days, collection discipline needs work.

How do I estimate the bad debt reserve?

Apply increasing loss percentages by bucket: ~1% on current, 5% on 31-60, 15% on 61-90, 30% on 91-120, 50% on 120+. Adjust based on your historical write-off experience.

What is AR concentration risk?

AR concentration is the % of total receivables owed by your top customers. If your top 5 represent more than 50%, a single customer's payment problem becomes your problem.

When should I send something to collections?

Try in-house collection for 90 days. After that, the probability of in-house recovery drops sharply. Third-party collections at day 90-120 typically recover 30-50% (minus a 20-35% commission).
