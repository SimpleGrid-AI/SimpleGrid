Customer contract risk - score the 12 red flags before you sign.

When a big customer sends 20 pages of legal terms, most manufacturers sign. This checklist flags the dangerous clauses and gives you exact counter-language to push back with.

For each clause below, mark the answer that best describes what the customer's contract says. The higher your risk score, the more you should push back before signing.

These clauses are negotiable - even with large customers

Never accept unlimited liability, consequential damages, or IP ownership of your processes.

"This is our standard contract" is a negotiating position, not a fact. The supplier who pushes back politely with specific counter-language gets the changes. The one who signs and complains later doesn't.

Why suppliers rarely push back - and why they should

The fear is losing the contract. The reality is that large customers expect supplier markups. Procurement teams budget for redlines. The contract you receive is a starting position, not a final offer. The clauses they care about most are different from the clauses they put in the document - the document is written by an outside law firm trying to protect against every possible scenario; what procurement actually cares about is price, lead time, and quality.

The five clauses worth fighting hardest on

  1. Limitation of liability - cap at 12 months of purchases. Unlimited liability is a deal-breaker; insurance does not cover everything.
  2. Consequential damages - exclude them. Your $50K part should not expose you to their $5M lost profits.
  3. IP ownership - they own the product; you keep the process. Process improvements are how you stay competitive.
  4. Termination for convenience - 90 days minimum, plus WIP and dedicated inventory protection.
  5. Audit rights - reasonable notice, limited frequency. Open-ended audit rights are an operational nightmare.
