Why Scope 1 + 2 is the right place to start

Scope 1 is the fuel you burn (natural gas, diesel, propane, refrigerant leakage). Scope 2 is the electricity you buy. Together they represent everything you directly control. Most OEM supplier ESG programs ask for Scope 1 + 2 first because the data exists - it's on your utility bills and fuel logs. Scope 3 (supply chain, purchased goods, employee travel, end-of-life) is harder because it requires data from your suppliers and customers. Our ESG readiness assessment walks the full reporting stack including governance, targets, and disclosure framework choice.

The state grid factor matters more than people realize

The same kWh emits 50x more carbon in Wyoming (1,872 lbs/MWh, coal-heavy) than Vermont (36 lbs/MWh, mostly hydro and nuclear). When OEMs evaluate site relocations or new supplier qualification, the grid emission factor of your state can swing the answer. If your facility is in a high-carbon state, a green tariff (where available) or onsite solar PPA is often the single biggest carbon reduction lever - and increasingly cost-neutral or net-positive in deregulated markets.

What "defensible" means for emissions data

For CDP, EcoVadis, or OEM supplier questionnaires, a defensible Scope 1 + 2 number means: utility bills as the activity source (not estimates), published emission factors (EPA eGRID for US grids, IEA for international, IPCC AR5 for refrigerant GWP), and a clear organizational boundary (operational vs equity vs financial control). For audit-ready reporting at scale (CSRD, SEC, SBTi), you'll need GHG Protocol Corporate Standard methodology and limited or reasonable assurance from an independent verifier. Knowing your equipment-level energy consumption is the bridge between operations data and emissions reporting.

Frequently asked questions

What are Scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions?

Scope 1 is direct emissions from sources you own or control: natural gas combustion, diesel and gasoline for fleet vehicles and forklifts, propane, refrigerant leakage. Scope 2 is indirect emissions from purchased electricity, heat, or steam - the emissions happen at the power plant, but you caused them. Scope 3 is everything else in your value chain: purchased materials, supplier emissions, employee commuting, business travel, end-of-life of your products. This tool covers Scope 1 + 2, which is what most OEM supplier programs ask for first.

Where do the state grid emission factors come from?

The EPA's eGRID dataset publishes per-state generation-mix emission rates (lbs CO2 per MWh) annually. We use the 2022 release as a reasonable defensible default. Hydro-heavy states (Vermont, Washington, Idaho) are very low; coal-heavy states (Wyoming, West Virginia, Kentucky) are very high. Same kWh in Vermont vs Wyoming = 50x difference in carbon. If you have a green tariff or onsite solar PPA, use a market-based factor instead.

What about Scope 3 (supply chain)?

Scope 3 is harder - it requires data from your suppliers. For most manufacturers, Scope 3 is 70-90% of total footprint (raw materials dominate). OEMs typically ask for Scope 1+2 first, then Scope 3 categories 1 (purchased goods) and 4 (upstream transport). Start by mapping your top-spend suppliers and asking them for emissions data. The CDP Supply Chain program is the most common framework. SBTi requires Scope 3 if it's over 40% of total.

Who is asking manufacturers for this?

As of 2024-2025: most large auto OEMs (Ford, GM, Stellantis, Toyota), aerospace primes (Boeing, Airbus, Lockheed), big retail (Walmart Project Gigaton, Target), most Fortune 100 industrials. EU CSRD reporting kicked in 2024 and pulls supplier data through the value chain. SEC climate rule (US) is in litigation but most Fortune 500 are reporting voluntarily via CDP anyway. If you sell into any of these supply chains, the question is when - not if.

How do I reduce my carbon footprint cost-effectively?

Scope 2 (electricity) is usually the fastest lever: efficiency upgrades (LED lighting, VFD compressors), then renewable procurement (green tariff or PPA - often cost-neutral or saves money in deregulated markets). Scope 1: switch fleet diesel forklifts to electric (paid back in 3-5 years on fuel + maintenance), seal refrigerant leaks, electrify low-temp process heat with heat pumps. Most manufacturers can cut Scope 1+2 by 25-40% in 3 years with no carbon offsets and positive ROI.
