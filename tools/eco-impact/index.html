Why ECO cost is the line item nobody tracks

Engineering rooms approve ECOs every week. The drawing changes, the BOM updates, the rev letter bumps. What rarely happens: anyone adds up the obsolete inventory writedown, the new tooling spend, the customer requalification fees, the operator training hours, and the production line downtime during the transition. Each ECO looks like 20 engineering hours. In aggregate they are sometimes the third-largest unbudgeted cost on the P&L.

The five hidden ECO costs

Inventory writedown. Old version becomes obsolete - raw, WIP, finished. Sometimes reworkable; usually not entirely. Tooling. New dies, molds, jigs, fixtures - and the salvage value of the old ones (often zero). Requalification. Supplier first-article, customer PPAP/FAI - both expensive, both slow. Training. Every operator on every shift needs the new SOP walked. Disruption. The hours your line is changing over instead of running.

When to defer instead of approve

If the ECO is not safety-critical and current inventory has runway, the cheapest path is often "approve effective on next run after stock depletion." This converts the largest line - inventory scrap - to zero. Cost-reduction ECOs especially benefit from deferred effectivity: you keep the savings without eating the writedown.

Frequently asked questions

What about positive ECO benefits (cost reduction, quality improvement)?

This tool quantifies the cost side. Compare the total ECO cost against expected benefit (unit cost savings * annual volume, scrap reduction, warranty savings). Payback period = total ECO cost / annual benefit. Most cost-reduction ECOs target <12 month payback; quality/safety ECOs often need to proceed regardless.

How do I estimate disruption hours?

Look at past similar changeovers. Typical disruption: tooling swap 2-8 hours, new fixture qual 4-16 hours, line rebalance 8-40 hours. Multiply by your line's hourly throughput value (revenue/hr or contribution margin/hr) to get dollars.

What is PPAP and when is it required?

Production Part Approval Process - the automotive (and aerospace via FAI/AS9102) protocol for proving a changed part still meets requirements. Required when: changed material, changed process, changed supplier location, or any change designated "significant" by the customer. Cost: $2-25K per part depending on complexity.

Can ECOs be reversed?

Technically yes - issue a counter-ECO reverting to the old design. In practice it's painful: old tooling may be scrapped, new inventory in pipeline, suppliers re-quoted. Always do the full impact math up front rather than rely on reversibility.

Who should approve an ECO?

Cross-functional CCB (Change Control Board): engineering, manufacturing, quality, purchasing, sales/customer service. The dollar threshold for CCB review varies - common practice: $500 informal, $5K engineering manager, $25K CCB, $100K executive committee.
