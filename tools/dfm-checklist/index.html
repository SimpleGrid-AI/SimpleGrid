Why DFM is the highest-leverage hour in engineering

Every dollar spent designing a part commits roughly ten to its production. Once a drawing is released, the cost structure is baked in - your shop can only optimize within the envelope you gave them. That is why a 30-minute DFM checklist at concept freeze is worth a 30-day redesign after PPAP. The cheapest mistake is the one caught before it leaves CAD.

The 10x rule

Cost of changing a design at concept: $1. At prototype: $10. At tooling: $100. At production launch: $1,000. After parts ship to the field: $10,000. The numbers are illustrative but the curve is real - every stage roughly 10x's the cost of a change. DFM is the only way to push decisions left on that curve.

Process-specific DFM matters

Generic DFM lists give you nothing useful for a real part. CNC has different rules than injection molding. Sheet metal has different rules than welding. This checklist gives you process-specific items - the ones your shop actually flags on FAI - so the conversation between design and manufacturing is structured, not adversarial.

Frequently asked questions

Who should fill out a DFM checklist - the engineer or the shop?

Both. The design engineer fills it first as a self-check. Then the shop (or contract manufacturer) reviews and flags items where their specific tooling, machines, or processes diverge from the assumptions. The conversation that follows is where most cost gets removed.

When in the design process should DFM be done?

At concept freeze - before detailed drawings. The cost of changing a design jumps 10x at each stage: concept ($1), prototype ($10), tooling ($100), production ($1,000), field ($10,000). A 30-minute DFM review at concept is worth a 30-day redesign at production.

What is the difference between DFM and DFA?

DFM (Design for Manufacturing) optimizes individual part production - tolerances, materials, machining time. DFA (Design for Assembly) optimizes how parts come together - fastener count, orientation, sub-assembly testability. DFMA is the combined practice. Use both.

Can DFM apply to legacy designs?

Yes - this is called DFM remediation or value engineering. Pick your top 10 highest-volume or highest-cost parts, run DFM on each, and prioritize changes by cost-savings-vs-requalification-cost. Typical ROI is 5-25% unit cost reduction.

How much can DFM really save?

Industry studies (Boothroyd Dewhurst, NIST) put DFM/DFA savings at 20-60% of unit cost when applied at concept. Lower for already-mature designs (5-15%). The biggest wins come from eliminating parts, loosening unnecessary tolerances, and switching to standard hardware.
