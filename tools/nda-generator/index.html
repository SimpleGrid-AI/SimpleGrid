When you actually need an NDA

Any time you share drawings, specs, pricing, process details, or customer lists with anyone outside your company. New customer requesting a quote on a custom part. Potential vendor reviewing your supplier requirements. Contract employee with floor access. Investor doing diligence. Equipment vendor walking through your facility. The list is long, and the cost of getting this wrong is a competitor with your information.

Mutual vs one-way

Mutual is the default for vendor and partner conversations - both sides may exchange information. One-way is for situations where only one side is sharing, like an investor or buyer doing diligence on you. Most attorneys will tell you "use mutual unless there's a reason not to" because it's faster to sign.

Frequently asked questions

Is this a substitute for an attorney?

No. This is a template that covers standard NDA terms. Your specific situation may need additional clauses (export controls, GDPR, industry-specific requirements). Have your attorney review before you sign.

How long should the NDA last?

3 years is the most common term for general business NDAs. Longer for sensitive trade secrets (5-7 years). Confidentiality should survive the term itself - typically 2-5 years post-termination.

What about trade secrets?

Trade secrets remain protected as long as they remain secret under state law. Make this explicit in the NDA: "obligations with respect to trade secrets continue for as long as the information qualifies as a trade secret."

Should I include non-solicitation?

Yes if the conversation involves walking the other party through your operation, exposing them to your key employees. No if it's a brief vendor evaluation. Non-solicit is enforceable in most states for 12 months.
