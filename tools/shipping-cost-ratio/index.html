Why this single ratio tells you more than any freight invoice

Your CFO knows revenue. Your shipping clerk knows freight. Almost no one connects the two. Freight as a percentage of revenue is the single most actionable freight KPI - it normalizes spend against business size, lets you benchmark against peers, and quantifies what a 1-point improvement is worth in real dollars. A $20M manufacturer running 8% freight ratio is leaving $400K+ a year on the table compared to a peer running 6%. That's not freight savings - that's net margin.

Why the ratio creeps up without anyone noticing

Three drivers, all silent. Fuel surcharges compound: when diesel jumps 30%, your 28% fuel surcharge becomes 36% and nobody renegotiates. Order mix shifts: your largest customer moves from full-pallet quantities to half-pallet replenishment - now it's all LTL at 2x the per-CWT cost. Accessorial creep: liftgate, residential, inside delivery, detention. Each adds $50-$150 per shipment and most don't make it into the rate sheet you negotiated. Run this ratio quarterly and you'll catch all three.

The four optimization moves that actually work

1) Carrier rebid every 12 months. Easy 10-20% on contracted lanes. 2) Consolidate inbound. Push suppliers to ship on your bill of lading and let your TMS optimize. 3) Mode shift discipline. Anything over 10,000 lbs runs FTL; under 150 lbs runs parcel; the middle stays LTL only if density justifies it. 4) Freight audit on contingency. 3-5% of total spend is overbilled. The audit service takes a cut of recoveries - you can't lose. Together these moves typically pull 1-2 full points off the freight ratio in year one.

Frequently asked questions

What's a normal freight-to-revenue ratio?

For US manufacturers, freight typically runs 3-7% of revenue. World-class (high-density, optimized network) sits under 3%. Above 7% usually indicates inefficiency - too many LTL shipments instead of consolidated FTL, untenable carrier rates, or a product with low value density (foam, lumber, pre-finished cabinets). E-commerce and B2C manufacturers often run 10-15% due to parcel-heavy mix.

Should inbound and outbound be tracked separately?

Yes - they tell different stories. Inbound freight (suppliers to you) shows up in COGS and affects gross margin. Outbound freight (you to customer) is either expensed or recovered through freight billed on the invoice. Tracking them separately surfaces opportunities: inbound is usually consolidatable across POs; outbound is dictated by customer order patterns. Audit which suppliers you're paying freight to, and whether you'd save by switching to FCA/FOB origin with your own carrier.

Does this include accessorial charges?

Yes - include all accessorials (fuel surcharge, liftgate, residential, inside delivery, detention) in your freight totals. Accessorials often add 30-50% to base freight. If you're only tracking base rates, you're underreporting freight cost. The freight invoice line items - not the rate sheet - are the source of truth.

How often should I rebid carriers?

Annual rebid is industry standard for LTL and FTL contracts. Parcel agreements can be renegotiated every 12-18 months. If your volume has grown 25%+ or your lanes have shifted, rebid earlier. Carriers expect rebids; not asking is leaving money on the table. Use a 3PL or freight broker for a benchmark even if you stay direct.

Are freight audit services worth it?

Yes, almost always. Freight audit firms (Sifi, AFS, nVision, etc.) recover an industry-average 3-5% of total freight spend in overcharges, duplicate billing, and rate misapplications. They charge a percentage of recoveries (typically 25-50% of found errors), so it's risk-free. For any manufacturer spending more than $500K/yr on freight, an audit pays for itself in month one.
