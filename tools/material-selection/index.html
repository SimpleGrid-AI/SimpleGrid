The two ways material selection goes wrong

Over-specifying is the more expensive failure mode. A part that needs to hold 10 ksi gets specified in 17-4 PH stainless because someone in the chain wasn't sure - now you're paying 6x the material cost and 3x the machining time for properties the application never uses. Under-specifying is the more dangerous failure mode. A part that sees fatigue loading gets specified in A36 because it was on the shelf - and fails in service. The right material is the cheapest one that comfortably meets every actual requirement, with a reasonable safety margin.

What this tool ranks against

Six criteria: tensile strength (matched to your load type), corrosion resistance (matched to your exposure), cost per pound, density (matched to your weight priority), machinability (matched to your machining priority), and maximum service temperature. Each material gets a 0-100 fit score. Materials that fail a hard requirement (temperature limit, food contact, load floor) are disqualified outright and shown in a separate list with the reason - so you can see what you ruled out and why.

Total cost of a material decision

Cost per pound is only one input. 316 stainless costs 35% more than 303 per pound, but machines about 3x slower - so the total cost per part is often higher even though the raw material is the headline number. Conversely, 6061 aluminum costs 5x what carbon steel does per pound but weighs 65% less - on a per-part basis the cost gap shrinks dramatically. Always tie the material choice to your real part weight and your real machining cycle time, not the per-pound list price.

Frequently asked questions

How are these scores calculated?

Each material gets a 0-100 fit score averaged across six criteria: strength (vs your load type), corrosion (vs your exposure), cost, weight, machinability, and temperature. Each criterion is weighted by the priority you set. Materials that fail a hard requirement (temperature limit, food contact) are disqualified outright and excluded from the ranking.

Why is 316 SS more expensive than 304?

316 contains 2-3% molybdenum, which dramatically improves corrosion resistance in chloride environments (saltwater, marine, pharma, chemical). Moly is expensive. 304 handles most general food and indoor humid environments fine - 316 is the right call only when you actually need the chloride resistance. Most kitchens and indoor pharma applications use 304 and save 30-40%.

What about composites and exotic alloys?

Carbon fiber, fiberglass, Inconel, Hastelloy, and similar materials are application-specific and pricing varies wildly with form factor (plate, tube, layup). This tool covers the 30+ most common manufacturing materials that cover roughly 95% of decisions in fab shops, machine shops, and product design. For exotics, get supplier quotes early - lead times are 6-12 weeks.

Should I always pick the cheapest material that meets specs?

Almost always yes, with two caveats. First, factor in total cost including machining time - 316 stainless costs 10% more than 303 in material but cuts 3x slower, so total part cost is often higher. Second, brand and field reputation matter - if your customer expects stainless and you ship galvanized, you save 80% on material and lose the customer. Match the spec to the application, not just the cheapest sheet.

Does this account for availability / lead time?

No - prices and properties only. Common materials (1018, A36, 304, 6061, ABS, nylon) are usually in stock at distributors. Less common alloys (4140, 7075, PEEK, exotic stainless) often carry 1-4 week lead times. Always check stock with your supplier before locking in the design. Substituting an out-of-stock material under deadline pressure is where most cost overruns originate.
