Why NMFC class matters more than weight

LTL pricing has two main inputs: weight and freight class. Most shippers know weight to the pound but treat class as a guess. That guess can double the bill. A 600 lb shipment at class 65 might cost $280 on a regional lane; the same shipment misdeclared at class 125 costs around $520. Same truck, same miles, same fuel - the only difference is the three-digit number on the bill of lading. Get class right and freight stops being a wildcard line on every order.

How density-based classification works

The NMFC (National Motor Freight Classification, published by the NMFTA) sorts every LTL commodity into 18 classes from 50 to 500. The single biggest factor is density - pounds per cubic foot. A 50-pcf shipment (steel coil, dense metal parts) is class 50, the cheapest. A 1-pcf shipment (mattresses, foam, large light parts) is class 400 or 500, the most expensive. Carriers price by hundredweight (CWT), and the per-CWT rate scales steeply with class. The same physical shipment becoming one class lighter typically saves 15-25% per shipment.

How to drop a class without changing the product

Pack tighter. Flat-pack what you assemble. Nest stackable parts. Remove dunnage that does not protect the load. Re-engineer the carton to fit the SKU instead of using a one-size-fits-all box. Palletize at the tightest cube the product allows. Every 1 pcf gained in density tightens the bracket and can drop you a class. The biggest wins come from products with poor fill ratios - assembled furniture, oversized cartons, single-product pallets with significant air.

When class is overridden by NMFC item number

Some commodities have a specific NMFC item number that fixes the class regardless of density. Mattresses, tires, plastic articles, certain chemicals - the tariff assigns a class based on handling, stowability, or liability, not density. If you ship a high-volume SKU, look up its NMFC item number in the official tariff (NMFTA subscription) or ask your carrier for the recommended item. Once you have it on file, every BOL uses the same item and you eliminate reclass risk.

Frequently asked questions

What is NMFC freight class?

The National Motor Freight Classification (NMFC) assigns every LTL commodity one of 18 classes from 50 (densest, cheapest) to 500 (lightest, most expensive). Class is determined primarily by density (lbs per cubic foot), with adjustments for handling, stowability, and liability. Lower class = cheaper per CWT.

How do I lower my freight class?

Increase density. Flat-pack, nest, palletize tighter, eliminate dunnage, remove void space, ship knocked-down rather than assembled. A class 100 shipment that becomes class 70 saves roughly 25% on LTL freight.

Does this work for international ocean freight?

No. NMFC class is a domestic US LTL trucking standard. International ocean and air freight use different systems (volumetric weight for air, CBM for ocean FCL/LCL). Use this tool only for US domestic LTL shipments.

Do I need to declare the exact NMFC item number?

On a bill of lading, yes - carriers expect a description and ideally an NMFC item number. Density-based class is a strong default, but some commodities have a specific NMFC item that overrides density. Check the NMFTA tariff (subscription) for your SKUs, or ask your carrier for the recommended item number.

What happens if I pick the wrong class?

Carriers inspect and reclassify. If your declared class is too low, you get a reclass charge - the rate difference plus a reweigh/inspection fee ($25-$100). Repeat reclasses can also trigger contract penalties. If your declared class is too high, you simply overpaid. Either way, accuracy pays.
