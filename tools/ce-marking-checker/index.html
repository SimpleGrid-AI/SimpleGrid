What CE marking actually means

CE is not a quality mark or a safety mark - it's a manufacturer's self-declaration that the product complies with all applicable EU directives. The CE mark goes on the product after the manufacturer (or its EU Authorized Representative) signs the Declaration of Conformity. For most directives the manufacturer can self-certify after applying harmonized standards; for higher-risk products (most medical devices, pressure equipment Cat II+, certain machinery in Annex IV) a Notified Body must perform conformity assessment. EU customs can and does stop products without proper CE marking.

The biggest mistakes US manufacturers make

Assuming UL = CE. UL is North American; CE is European. They're not interchangeable. Skipping the Authorized Representative. Non-EU manufacturers must appoint an EU AR for most directives - your distributor doesn't count unless contracted. Forgetting RoHS. Almost any electrical product needs RoHS even if not "electronic." Outdated technical files. The file must reflect the current product - design changes require updates and often re-test.
