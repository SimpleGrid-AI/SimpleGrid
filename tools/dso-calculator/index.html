What DSO actually measures

DSO answers one question: "After I issue an invoice, how long does it take for the cash to arrive?" If you bill $4M a quarter and have $2M in AR, your DSO is 45 days - on average, every dollar billed sits in receivables for 45 days before becoming cash.

The reason this matters: DSO drives working capital, and working capital drives cash flow. A 15-day DSO improvement on a $20M revenue business frees roughly $820K in cash - permanently. Once. That cash never has to be earned again.

Why customers pay slowly

  • You invoice slowly. Five-day invoicing delays add five days to DSO mechanically. The customer doesn't even have to do anything.
  • The invoice is wrong. Missing PO numbers, wrong contact, wrong line items - any of these reset the clock to the customer's next AP cycle.
  • They forgot. Most overdue invoices are not refusals - they are oversights. A polite reminder at day 5 solves a remarkable percentage of them.
  • They use you as free credit. Some customers pay every supplier exactly when called - and not before. The ones who never call get paid never.
  • They have cash problems. A persistent slow payer with no specific dispute is the early signal of customer financial distress. Investigate before they file Chapter 11 with your invoice unpaid.

The compounding effect

DSO improvement is the rare lever that improves three things at once: cash balance (one-time bump), interest expense (lower line-of-credit utilization, recurring), and bad debt risk (older AR = higher write-off probability). Banks reward it with better terms. PE buyers reward it with higher multiples. Your own balance sheet rewards it with literal cash you can deploy.

Frequently asked questions

What is a good DSO for a manufacturer?

US manufacturing average is 45-55 days. Best-in-class is 30-40 days. If your stated terms are Net 30 and your DSO is 60+, customers are paying 30 days late on average - and every one of those days is cash you have earned but cannot use.

DSO vs. Best Possible DSO - what is the difference?

DSO measures total AR including overdue. Best Possible DSO (BPDSO) considers only current (non-overdue) AR. The gap between the two is your collection inefficiency - cash you should have already collected but haven't.

Should I offer prompt-pay discounts?

Sometimes. A 2/10 net 30 discount costs you 2% of revenue but typically pulls DSO in by 15-20 days. The math works if your cost of capital is above ~36% annualized - true for distressed companies, rarely true for healthy ones.

How do I improve DSO without losing customers?

Five levers, in order: 1) Invoice the day you ship, not weekly. 2) Email invoices, never mail. 3) Confirm receipt within 48 hours and resolve disputes within 5 days. 4) Call past-due accounts at day 5 (not day 30). 5) Run a weekly AR aging review with sales involved.

Why is DSO sometimes called the "silent margin killer"?

Because slow collections cost you the same as a price discount - but invisibly. If your cost of capital is 8% and DSO grows from 45 to 60 days, you have effectively given every customer a 0.33% price cut on their entire annual purchase.
