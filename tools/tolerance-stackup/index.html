Why tolerance stack-ups bite you on the line

A perfectly toleranced part stacks with five other perfectly toleranced parts and the assembly jams. Each component met its drawing - the stack did not. Worst-case math assumes the universe conspires against you (every dimension at its extreme, simultaneously). RSS math accepts that real production is statistically distributed. The right tolerance is almost always somewhere between the two answers, sized to your real process capability.

Worst case vs RSS in one sentence

Worst case adds tolerances; RSS adds tolerance squares and takes the square root. Worst case is what a contracts lawyer wants. RSS is what a manufacturing engineer with real SPC data uses. RSS typically yields about 60-70 percent of the worst-case stack number, which often turns a failing assembly into a passing one without changing a single drawing.

How to tighten the right dimension

If your stack fails, look at the contribution table. The dimension with the largest RSS contribution percentage is your lever. Tightening a dimension that contributes 60 percent of the stack from ±0.005" to ±0.003" can save the assembly. Tightening one that contributes 4 percent buys almost nothing and costs you in machining. Always tighten the biggest contributor first - and only as much as you need.

Frequently asked questions

When should I use worst-case vs RSS?

Use worst case for safety-critical assemblies (medical, aerospace, life safety) where any failure is unacceptable. Use RSS (root-sum-square) for cost-sensitive production where the statistical probability of all dimensions hitting the extreme is vanishingly small. RSS gives roughly 3-sigma coverage (99.73 percent of assemblies) and typically allows about 1.5-2x looser individual tolerances than worst case for the same assembly spec.

Does RSS apply to non-normal distributions?

RSS assumes each dimension is normally distributed and independent. Real machining processes are mostly normal-ish, but tool wear, fixture drift, and operator habits introduce skew. If your process has Cpk below 1.33 or visible non-normal patterns, treat RSS as optimistic and add a safety factor of 1.2-1.5x to the RSS tolerance.

What is Cp/Cpk and how does it relate?

Cp is the ratio of tolerance band to 6x process standard deviation (capability if centered). Cpk is the same but penalized if the mean is off-center. Stack-up assumes a Cpk of about 1.0 (3 sigma fits the tolerance). If your real Cpk is 1.33+ your RSS is conservative; if Cpk is below 1.0 your RSS understates real variation - tighten the spec or fix the process.

Can I use Monte Carlo instead?

Yes, and you should for non-linear assemblies, geometric stacks (GD&T), or non-normal distributions. Monte Carlo runs 10,000+ simulated assemblies sampling each dimension from its real distribution. This tool does worst-case and RSS which are sufficient for linear 1D stack-ups. For 2D/3D geometric stacks use a tool like Sigmetrix or Minitab.

How do I tighten the right dimension?

Look at RSS contribution percentage. The dimension contributing the largest fraction of the stack tolerance is your biggest lever. Tightening it from a half-tolerance is what shrinks the stack the most. Tightening the smallest contributor wastes money. The contribution table in this tool sorts contributors by impact for exactly this reason.
