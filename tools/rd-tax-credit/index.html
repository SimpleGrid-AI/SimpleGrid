R&D Tax Credit Estimator.

Most mid-market manufacturers qualify for the federal R&D tax credit. Only about 5% claim it - because they think "R&D" means lab coats. In reality, developing custom tooling, improving processes, and solving technical problems all qualify. Typical claim: $20K-$200K per year.

Qualifying R&D activities checklist

Check every activity your employees regularly perform. Most manufacturers underestimate this - if any of these happen at your shop, you likely qualify.

Qualified research expenses (current year)

Wages: only the portion of time spent on qualifying activities (e.g., an engineer at 60% qualifying time and $120K salary contributes $72K). Supplies: materials consumed in prototyping or testing, not regular production materials. Contract research: 65% of payments to outside contractors counts.

Prior 3 years QREs (for ASC method)

If you haven't tracked QREs separately before, enter 0 in all three prior years - the ASC formula will treat this as a first-time claim with a higher effective rate.

Why most manufacturers miss this

The R&D tax credit was created in 1981 to keep American innovation onshore. Congress made it permanent in 2015. It's worth roughly 6-14% of qualifying spend, as a dollar-for-dollar federal tax credit (better than a deduction). Yet only ~5% of eligible US small and mid-sized companies claim it.

The reason: the IRS-defined term "Research & Development" is much broader than the colloquial meaning. Section 41's four-part test asks (1) is the work technological in nature, (2) does it serve a business purpose, (3) is there technical uncertainty at the start, and (4) is uncertainty resolved through systematic experimentation? Process improvement, custom tooling, materials substitution, and new-product development all routinely meet this test in a manufacturing environment.

The look-back opportunity

You can amend the past three years' federal returns to claim missed credits. For a typical mid-market manufacturer that has been doing engineering, NPI, and process improvement work the whole time, this is often a $100K-$500K refund check. The IRS gives back what was always yours.

State credits stack on top. About 35 states offer their own R&D credit, ranging from 5% to 20% of QREs. California, Texas, New York, and Illinois are particularly generous.

The substantiation problem

The credit is yours by statute. The risk is documentation. The IRS wants project-level records: which employee worked on which qualifying project, how many hours, on what dates, what technical uncertainty was being resolved. Most manufacturers don't track this granularly - which is why a specialist study is usually worth the fee.

A good specialist will interview your engineers and production managers, reconstruct project timelines from QC reports and ECNs, and produce a defensible study. Self-filed claims without substantiation are the ones that get disallowed in audit.

Frequently asked questions

Does my manufacturing business qualify for the R&D credit?

Most do. The credit covers developing new products, improving manufacturing processes, designing custom tooling, testing new materials, and developing automation software. You do NOT need scientists in lab coats.

How far back can I claim the credit?

Three years - you can amend prior-year returns to claim credits you missed. Many manufacturers find $50K-$200K in unclaimed credits in a look-back study.

What is the simplified ASC method?

The Alternative Simplified Credit method gives you a 14% credit on the amount by which current QREs exceed 50% of the average of the prior three years' QREs. Simpler than the regular method and works for first-time claimers.

Can I use the credit if I'm not profitable?

Sometimes. Small businesses (gross receipts under $5M, less than 5 years old) can offset payroll tax up to $500K/year - usable even at a loss. Otherwise the credit carries forward 20 years.

Do I need a specialist or can my regular CPA claim it?

Strongly recommend a specialist for the first claim. Specialists typically identify 2-3x more qualifying activities than self-studies, and their fees are usually contingent on the credit found.
