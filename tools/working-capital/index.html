The hidden cost of growth

The most common surprise for a growing mid-market manufacturer: profitable years that leave the bank account empty. Working capital is the explanation. Every new customer order adds inventory and receivables before it generates cash. A company growing 30% per year at 20% NWC/Revenue needs to fund roughly 6% of revenue in new working capital every year - cash that does not come back until the growth slows.

This is why fast-growing companies routinely raise debt or equity even though they are profitable. The P&L looks great. The cash flow statement tells a different story.

The three levers

  • Reduce DSO. Faster invoicing (same day, not weekly), weekly AR review, prompt-pay discounts (2/10 net 30), credit checks before opening accounts, automated dunning. Pair with the DSO Calculator and AR Aging Analyzer.
  • Reduce DIO. Kill dead stock, tighten reorder points, run cycle counts to catch phantom inventory, push toward smaller more frequent batches. Pair with the Dead Stock Identifier and Reorder Point Calculator.
  • Extend DPO. Negotiate longer terms with suppliers - Net 45 or Net 60 instead of Net 30. Use scale where you have it. But don't pay late: late-paying customers become the worst suppliers' favorite target for price increases.

Frequently asked questions

What is a healthy working capital ratio?

For manufacturers, 1.5x to 2.5x is healthy. Below 1.2x signals liquidity risk. Above 3.0x usually means too much cash is locked in inventory or AR that could be deployed elsewhere.

What is the cash conversion cycle?

CCC = DSO + DIO - DPO. It measures how many days cash is tied up in operations between paying suppliers and collecting from customers. Manufacturing average is 45-65 days. Best-in-class is 25-35 days.

Why does growth eat working capital?

Every dollar of new revenue requires roughly 15-30 cents in additional inventory and receivables. To grow 20%, a manufacturer with $5M in working capital and $20M in revenue needs another $1M tied up. That cash has to come from somewhere - retained earnings, debt, or equity.

What is the fastest way to reduce CCC?

Three levers, in order of typical impact: 1) Reduce DSO by tightening collections. 2) Reduce DIO by killing dead stock and tightening reorder points. 3) Extend DPO by negotiating longer terms with suppliers. The first two are usually faster wins.

Is too little working capital a problem?

Yes. Negative or near-zero working capital means current liabilities exceed current assets - you cannot fund day-to-day operations without external financing. It is the most common reason profitable companies file for bankruptcy.
