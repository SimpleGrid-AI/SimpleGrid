The story

Building SimpleGrid.

How a factory that survived two failed ERPs became the blueprint for a system that finally fits the floor.

01

A factory that ran on memory.

Before SimpleGrid, our founder ran a $30M manufacturing business - multi-stage production, hundreds of workers, jobs moving across the floor every hour of every day. On paper, the books were clean. On the floor, no one could answer a simple question: where is job 4521 right now, and will it ship on time?

The real status lived in one person's head, a spreadsheet version nobody fully trusted, and a dozen WhatsApp threads. It worked - until that one person was on leave, or a buyer called, or month-end arrived and the numbers didn't match the floor.

02

The first ERP.

So we did exactly what everyone tells you to do: we bought an ERP. Months of implementation. A six-figure bill. A consultant who had never set foot on a factory floor.

The system was built for a thousand businesses, not ours. Every small change - a new approval rule, a buyer-specific QC gate - became a multi-week change request. And the people who actually do the work never logged in. Within a year it was a very expensive database that nobody updated.

03

The second ERP.

We told ourselves we'd simply picked the wrong vendor. So we tried again - a different platform, a bigger promise, a fresh implementation. Same months. Same bill. Same result.

Two ERP implementations. Both failed. We weren't unlucky: roughly 75% of mid-market ERP projects fail or get quietly abandoned. We were the rule, not the exception.

04

Back to spreadsheets.

With two failures behind us, we ran a multi-stage factory the only way that actually worked: Google Sheets and group chats. Fragile, manual, and entirely dependent on one person remembering everything.

But here's the thing - the floor used it. Because it spoke their language. No menus, no codes, no eight-tab forms. Just type what happened.

05

The problem, in one sentence.

That contradiction was the whole answer. ERPs don't fail because they're too simple. They fail because they're built for the enterprise, not your floor - and the floor never adopts them.

The books were never the problem. The floor was invisible.

We didn't need a bigger system. We needed an operations layer that sat on top of the accounting we already used, and that the floor would actually pick up on day one.

06

So we're building it ourselves.

The problem we lived is the one we now solve. SimpleGrid is the Factory Ops Cloud - jobs, batches, BOMs, QC checkpoints and costing, live in one place, sitting on top of the QuickBooks or Tally you already run. No migration. No rip-and-replace.

And because we were the customer first, we prove it before we ask you to commit: we configure it to your floor at our cost, you run it live for 30 days, and you pay only when it works.

It's the system we wished we had. Now it's yours.
